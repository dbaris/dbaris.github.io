STITCH IT TOGETHER

From landscapes split by geopolitical lines to the individual psychological experience of blocked mobility, the experience of the border condition traverses every scale of the built environment. As symbolic objects, borders present false binaries between danger and safety, interior and exterior, inclusion and exclusion. Borders are highly politicized and hyper-surveilled sites, typically represented as opaque lines on a map or floor plan. This linear condition appears solid and permanent, yet it is always open to possibilities of change or destruction.

Stitch it Together challenges the narrative of the border as permanent, binary, and impervious through a critique of traditional representational practices at the scale of the body, the building, the landscape, and the digital. The work is sited across the charged space of the US-Mexico border line, which splits a continental tapestry intimately connected by ecology, infrastructure, and migratory systems. Alternative counter-mapping proposals offer the construction of new frameworks for an interconnected and interdependent region. A distributed textile collective forms the production model for a series of designed political objects to soften and subvert the border condition at the scale of human, infrastructural, and celestial bodies. In equal embrace of technology and craft, these objects lean into opportunities for disruption as opened by the technological glitch.
